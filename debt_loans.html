1. Federal Student Loans

Federal student loans are student loans that are guarenteed by the federal government. The government offers different types of student loans. Some of the loans have a total loan limit which means that your loan ammounts accumulate to a total amount and once that limit is reached, you are no longer eligible for federal student loans.
  • Subsidized Loans
    • Direct subsudized loans do not accrue interest while an individual is in school or during their 6 month grace period while they are our of school or graduate.

  • Direct Unsubsidized Loans
    • Direct unsubsidized loans acrue interest from the first day you receive them. In other words, these federal loans accummulate interest while you are in school.

  • Direct PLUS Loans
    • According to StudentAid.gov, Direct PLUS loans are for graduate or professional students AND parents of dependent undergraduate students to help pay for education expenses not covered by other financial aid. Unlike Federal Subsudized and Unsubsidized Loans, Direct PLUS loans are not based upon financial need.

  • Consolidation Loans
    • Direct consolidated loans is the term given for federal loans combined into one loan so that the payee is able to make one payment instead of multiple loan payments. This option typically occurs after your 6-month grace period or 6-months after you graduate.
Visit StudentAid.gov to learn more about which Federal loan is best for you.

2. Private

Private student loans are offered by private institutions and typically have a higher interest rate and shorter time period to pay back the loans.

Unlike Federal subsidized and Federal unsibsidized loans, private student loans are not based upon financial need and require a credit check. If you are a student with little to no credit history, you will most likely need a co-signer to help you secure a private student loan.

1. New Car Loans

A new car loan is a loan for a new vehicle. You would be the first owner of the vehicle. Typically, new vehicles have a lower interest rate, however, they typically cost more at the time of purchase.

2. Used Car Loans

Used car loans apply to vehicles that have had at least one owner. Used vehicles have a higher interest rate, however, they typically cost less at the time of purchase.

When purchasing a used vehicle, you should inspect the vehicle's past history and mechanics if possible. For example, is the vehicle leaking oil, do all the doors and locks work, does the vehicle need new tires soon, was the vehicle previously in an accident and fixed up, etc? Oftentimes, you can ask the dealership for the cars history before you purchase the used vehicle.

3. Auto loan refinancing

If your credit score is better anytime after you purchase your and you want to get a better interest rate on your vehicle loan, you can ask your bank/credit union (or a different bank/credit union) to refinance your auto loan.

Generally, the time to wait to refinance a loan is six months from the start of your loan. Many times, you are not able to refinance your auto loan if you are upside down on your loan. Upside down means that you owe more for the vehicle than the vehicle is worth.

4. Leasing a vehicle

Leasing a vehicle is when you have a contract to rent a car for a specified period of time from the dealership.You do not own the vehicle. When the period of time is up, you can look into options to purchase the vehicle you were leasing, or lease another vehicle.

1. Conventional Loans

Conventional loans are your typical mortgage loans.

Typically requires:
  • Credit score 620 or higher
  • Down payment of at least 3%

2. Federal Housing Administration (FHA) Loans

The Federal Housing Administration is a part of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). FHA insures loans for low income and individuals with lower credit ratings so that lenders can provide more options for the applicant.

Typically requires:
  • Credit score of 500 with a 10% down payment
  • Credit score of 580 with a 3.5% down payment

3. Veterans Affairs (VA) Loans

VA loans are loans guaranteed by the VA to veterans, service members, and their survivors obtain loans. The VA assists eligble individuals with buy, build, improve, or refinance homes. An individual can only use one VA loan at a time.

If you want to know if you qualify for a VA loan, you can follow this link for eligibilty requirements.
    https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/eligibility/

4. U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Loans

The USDA Loans are guaranteed by the USDA, and are generally available in rural and suburban areas. You can contact your real estate agent to see if you are eligible for this type of loan.

According to Zillow.com, some of the requirements for a USDA loan are:
  • Open country and not part of, or associated with an urban area
  • A town, village, city or place that has a population of less than 10,000 people
  • Or, a town, village, city or place that is not within a highly-populated metropolitan area (meaning the population must be above 10,000 but below 20,000)

Payday loans are a type of short-term loan (as in your next pay check) where a lender will give you a very high-interest rate to pay pack the loan. These loans are typically based upon your income as opposed to your credit score. These loans usually have you pay the amount in full plus the interest that was accrued with your next paycheck. This can be dangerous and can continue a cycle of not having any money for expenses with your next paycheck.
Debt consolidation loans are loans that are for a specified amount and are used to pay of your other loans so that you can have a lower monthly payment, and if possible, a lower interest rate. Debt consolidation loans can be secured or unsecured loans. Secured loans usually have lower interest rates than unsecured loans and are more likely to be approved.
  • Secured loan:
    This means you offer the bank/credit union collateral to secure the loan. Typically, the collateral is a house or vehicle that is fully owned by you. If you don't pay back the loan, the bank gets to keep your collateral.

  • Unsecured loan:
    An unsecured loan is where you do not have any collateral to offer the lender. Unsecured loans usually have a higher interest rate and a lower loan amount.

2. Amortization Schedule

    An amortization schedule is a table (and if available a chart) that shows how the amount of the monthly payment breaks down between the amount paid to the mortgage principal and the amount of the payment paid to the interest of the loan. This table allows a person to see how much they pay in interest over time, how their payment is being split and how their loan balance changes over time. Factors used to calculate the amortization table include the initial loan amount, closing costs that may increase the loan amount, and thereby the monthly payments. Interest rates affect the monthly total payment of the loan as well. The higher the interest rate, the higher the total payment. However, interest payment amounts are higher at the beginning of the loan repayment so that the lender is paid before more upfront in repayments.
